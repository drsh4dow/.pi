# Pitching: Hooks, Steam Pages, Trailers

Disclosed reference for [`game-design`](SKILL.md) — the selling branch.

## Hooks (Ryan Clark)

A hook is the surprising *and* desirable thing that makes a stranger stop scrolling — what makes the game worth abandoning their current favorite for. "Good game," "X but better," and "X but in space" are not hooks. A mechanical detail is not a hook unless it's legible in one sentence or one screenshot. Strong games stack multiple hooks. Evaluate a hook against the market: find the 5–10 closest comparable games, check their revenue and review counts, and judge whether this hook clears that field. If the hook can't be delivered in a sentence, an image, or three seconds of footage, it will fail at every touchpoint below — send the user back to design.

## Steam page (Zukowski)

- Genre first: players buy within their favorite genres. Tag and describe the game by its real genre comparables, and use **anchors** (instantly familiar genre signals — the Metroidvania map, the deckbuilder hand) so the shopper knows what shelf it's on at a glance.
- Short description = genre + hook in the first sentence. Reasons to play, not lore.
- Capsule art must communicate genre at thumbnail size; test it at 120px.
- Screenshots: gameplay with UI showing the hook, ordered by strength; first screenshot works as the ad.
- Page goes up as early as possible — it exists to farm wishlists into festivals (Next Fest is one-shot; time it), streamer coverage, and a demo. Demos are the current meta.

## Trailer (Derek Lieu)

Order is fixed: **Genre → Hook → Content.** Establish genre in the first shots (one or two can do it), the hook immediately after, and features/content only from the midpoint — content is a multiplier, and a multiplier on an unestablished game multiplies zero. Cold-open on the hook; assume autoplay, muted, three seconds of patience. Anchors up front make it familiar; a trailer that stays on anchors too long reads as a lesser copy of an older game. Structure the back half as escalation → climax; end on title + call to action (wishlist). 30 seconds of real gameplay beats 90 seconds of logos and slow pans — never open on studio cards or environment mood shots.

## Pitch critique bar

A pitch/page/trailer critique is complete when it names: the genre a stranger would guess in 3 seconds, the hook and where it first appears, each anchor used, and the single strongest change ranked first.
